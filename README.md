# The Motive Power Of Logic
Writings on personal adventures through independent research in modern science. Views of optimism and epistemological health in scientific and moral philosophy.

# Who Am I
My name is Zachary Mullaghy, and for the past year I have been dedicated to research in transform theory.

In March of 2025 I was working as a medical physicist. During a move from Las Vegas to Florida I discovered a way of stacking Hilbert curves of multiple orders to create an interleaved space that formed the backbone of what I now recognize as wavelets.

(If you had asked me at the time, I would have told you I had discovered a new transform. What I actually discovered was something better: the soil was already rich with theory thanks to the multiresolution analysis community.)

Now — and this is important — if you ever get the pleasure of doing something like this, of finding a side door into someone else's theory, pay attention to it. Take time to read their stories and understand their motivations. Empathizing with the passions of the people who built the mathematics helps later when that mathematics begins questioning your own understanding.

One moment that stayed with me was Daubechies' ten lectures on wavelets, a landmark scientific communication event that helped standardize wavelets in the form we largely use today.

At first, this rediscovery was interesting, but not “leave your career and bet everything on yourself” interesting.

What truly stuck came soon after.

I realized I had a method for building transforms from what I now understand as geometric filtrations. At the same time, my other long-standing obsession — Voronoi diagrams — turned out to provide exactly the geometric structure needed to explore the idea further.

That was the turning point.

If you ask me how it happened, I’ll tell you I got lucky in a big way.

Developing a generalizable framework of geometric filtrations led me to study a cohomological backbone for transform theory, which I am now attempting to formalize in Lean as part of a research direction I hope to call transform topology.

# Current Research

My current work focuses on PDE analysis in both linear and nonlinear systems using:

discrete exterior calculus

profinite identification through refinement levels

lattice exhaustions for constructing solutions

Much of this work currently sits within C*-functional analysis, where I am exploring how these ideas interact with operator-theoretic structures.

# What Constitutes "Verification"

The primary strength that you have as a researcher is that you can move fluidly and follow things fully. The risk is you dont have oversight. That is why your verification methods need to account for this. In todays world if you are not using LEAN (or a formal proof verification method) and python (or a numerical analysis platform) then you cannot be sure of your work. If you cannot be sure of your work you need to entertain the idea of your criticism more, which COULD mean you have false assumptions. This repository contains tons of my verifications and my visions for where this is going. Verification benefits by taking the user out of the loop so feel free to have at any of it!

# Philosophy as an Independent Scientist

It is one thing to find some math. From my experience though that is far from the hardest part. I want to get one thing across to anyone taking this path. You will be treated unfairly. I can almost promise you that. Managing your own insight in a rigorous field is not a small feat. We are imperfect logical creatures and we are prone to mistakes. That is why the loudest driving force in your work needs to be skepticism. No result survives without a verification pipeline.

What sets a person apart is not whether or not they've been treated unfairly or not. I know thats not what most people want to hear. Fair treatment is not a prerequisite to success. Logical consistency is the prerequisite for success. Serving that logic then - when you are treated unfairly you need to not harden you heart against it. Often times theres a nugget of truth in the unfair treatment.

If you allow yourself to adapt to criticism and not allow it to crush you then you have the mindset needed for independent research. This strikes me as difficult to teach. But the adage I always come back to for this is "it is the mark of an educated mind to entertain an idea without accepting it" 

This adaptability is a form of optimism that I wish was more present in the world. The motive power of logic to me boils down largely to the motive power of a pragmatic optimism.
